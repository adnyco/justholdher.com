The Story of Witness
Foster Care, Medicine, and Presence

“WOULD YOU, PLEASE… JUST HOLD HER UNTIL SHE DIES?”

That was the call.

Two months old. Three pounds.
We named her Jayden.

She arrived swaddled in hospital linens,
tethered to tubes and monitors.
Blind. Deaf. Insulin-dependent.
Hands and feet raw since birth.
Thirty diagnoses. No plan.

They didn’t ask us to save her.
They asked us to stay.

What followed wasn’t heroic. It was relentless.
Alarms in the dark. A thin-gauge lancet.
A cooler on the porch.
A spiral notebook filling with numbers.

And one question I kept asking, again and again:
What about breast milk?

Just Hold Her is the true story of a foster mother’s nightwatch — a literary memoir where medicine, bureaucracy, and human attachment collide, and where faith arrives less as certainty than as presence.

It stays with Jayden from that first call into the long months that followed, bearing witness to the quiet, aching grace at the edge of life — where protocol ends, and staying costs what it costs.

About the Author

JUDY WRIGHT

Judy Wright and her late husband, John, fostered fifty-five infants over twelve years, many medically fragile. In 2012, they were honored as both Oakland County and Michigan Foster Parents of the Year. Their advocacy helped inspire the John and Judy Wright Scholarship for Foster Youth and the Judy Wright Volunteer of the Year Award.

Just Hold Her is her debut memoir. A true account of a nightwatch that began with a single call to “just hold” a dying baby, and unfolded into a sustained act of care, witness, and staying.

Judy writes for foster, medical, and faith-aware communities about presence, caregiving, and the courage to remain when certainty is gone.

About the Book

JUST HOLD HER

When the call comes to “just hold her until she dies,” a foster mother opens her arms to a child the world has already given up on.

Built from the spiral notebook kept through long nights, Just Hold Her tells the story of Jayden... two months old, three pounds, and failing fast on prescribed formula. In the NICU, her foster mother asks the same question again and again: What about breast milk? Doctors see a chart. Caseworkers see regulations. One pediatrician finally sees a child... and writes the letter that changes everything.

A literary memoir of presence over protocol, Just Hold Her bears witness to caregiving at the edge of certainty, where faith flickers, systems strain, and staying becomes its own quiet act of defiance.
