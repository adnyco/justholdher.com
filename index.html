“Would you, please… just hold her until she dies?”

That was the call.

Two months old. Four pounds.
Blind. Deaf. Insulin-dependent.
Tethered to monitors. No plan.

They did not ask Judy Wright to save her.
They asked her to stay.

What followed was not heroic. It was relentless.
Alarms in the dark.
A heel prick every two hours.
A house rearranged around oxygen tanks, feedings, and numbers.

And beside it all, a spiral notebook.
Page after page.
A record of one small life, kept because the care required it.

Just Hold Her is a relentless, literary memoir of foster care, medical bureaucracy, and the quiet defiance of staying.

A witness story.
Not a rescue story.

About the Author

Judy Wright

She became a writer only because some things she witnessed demanded a record. Just Hold Her, drawn from the spiral notebooks she kept during one child’s long medical vigil, is her debut memoir.

Alongside her late husband, John, she fostered fifty-five children over twelve years, many of them medically fragile. Their home became a place where readiness was ordinary: bassinets brought up in the night, doctor visits folded into the week, and children held without any promise of what would happen next.

Judy Wright is, above all else, a mother.

About the Book

Just Hold Her

When a foster mother is asked to hold a baby until she dies, she says yes.

What begins as end-of-life care becomes something harder to name: a long vigil lived inside ordinary days, where maternal instinct, medical authority, and foster-care bureaucracy keep colliding.

Built from the notes she kept in spiral notebooks, Just Hold Her is both literary memoir and lived record. It traces what it costs to remain present when prognosis, policy, and exhaustion all press in.

For readers of literary memoir, caregiving narratives, and stories of love without guarantees, this is a book about witness, presence, and the quiet labor of beginning again.
