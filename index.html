“Would you, please… just hold her until she dies?”

That was the call.

Two months old. Four pounds.
Blind. Deaf. Insulin-dependent.
Tethered to monitors. No plan.

They did not ask Judy Wright to save her.
They asked her to stay.

What followed was not heroic. It was relentless.
Alarms in the dark.
A heel prick every two hours.
A house rearranged around oxygen tanks, feedings, and numbers.

And beside it all, a spiral notebook.
Page after page.
A record of one small life, kept because the care required it.

Just Hold Her is a relentless, literary memoir of foster care, medical bureaucracy, and the quiet defiance of staying.

A witness story.
Not a rescue story.

About the Author

Judy Wright

Alongside her late husband, John, Judy Wright fostered fifty-five children over twelve years in Oakland County, many of them medically fragile. Their home became a place where readiness was ordinary: bassinets carried up in the night, doctor visits folded into the week, and children held without any promise of what would happen next.

The work brought her inside hospitals, courtrooms, case plans, and the daily realities of caring for children whose lives were already marked by instability, medical trauma, and loss. In 2012, she and John were named Michigan Foster Parents of the Year by the Michigan Supreme Court.

She did not set out to become a writer. She became one because some things she witnessed could not go unwritten. Just Hold Her drawn from the spiral notebooks she kept during one child’s long medical vigil, is her debut memoir.

The memoir came later.
The work of staying came first.

Judy speaksa> to foster, medical, and faith-aware communities about presence, caregiving, and the courage required to remain available. She lives with the conviction that while children may not remember every face that held them, they remember what it felt like to be held.

About the Book

Just Hold Her

When a foster mother is asked to hold a baby until she dies, she says yes.

What begins as end-of-life care becomes something else. A long vigil, lived inside ordinary days. Maternal instinct, medical authority, and foster-care bureaucracy keep colliding. The work narrows to what can be done: feedings, numbers, alarms… and the decision to begin again.

Built from the spiral notebooks she kept in real time, Just Hold Her is a literary memoir and a record of care. It traces what it costs to remain present when prognosis, policy, and exhaustion all press in.

This is not a rescue story. It is a witness story… about presence, caregiving, and the quiet labor of staying.
