The Story

"Would you, please… just hold her until she dies?"

That was the call. Two months old. Four pounds. Blind, deaf, and tethered to monitors, the infant arrived with twenty-seven diagnoses and no plan. They didn't ask Judy Wright to save her. They asked her to stay.

Just Hold Her is the true story of a long vigil inside Michigan's foster care system. A quiet Oakland County house becomes a lifeline where neonatal medicine, child welfare bureaucracy, and human attachment collide — and where care is measured in alarms, feedings, phone calls, and the daily choice to show up again.

Built from contemporaneous spiral notebooks kept across 674 days of medical caregiving, the memoir traces an arc from fear to fierce attachment — and the cost of staying without guarantees. At its center is a foster mother navigating a system that had already prepared for a different outcome, and asking the one question no one else was still asking: What about breast milk?

This is not a rescue story. It is a witness story — for caregivers, foster parents, and the medical professionals who live at the edge of what medicine can do.

Key Facts for Press

The Document

Primary-source medical journals

The memoir is reconstructed from contemporaneous spiral notebooks containing hand-drawn grids of glucose readings, insulin injections, feedings, and oxygen levels — recorded every two hours across 674 days. The grid is the book's central visual motif and its documentary foundation: a record kept not for publication, but because the care required it.

The Placement

Oakland County, Michigan foster care

The infant arrived with twenty-seven diagnoses including congenital syphilis, insulin dependence, probable blindness, probable deafness, and failure to thrive. The medical system had prepared for her death. The system had no plan beyond the placement.

The Question

"What about breast milk?"

Against institutional protocol and repeated dismissal, Wright pursued donor breast milk as a medical intervention — a question that put her in direct conflict with hospital authority and became the pivot of the story and the outcome.

The Recognition

Michigan Supreme Court, 2012

Judy and John Wright were named Michigan's Foster Parents of the Year — recognized by the Michigan Supreme Court and the Oakland County Board of Commissioners — following twelve years of foster care and fifty-five placements, many of them medically complex.

The Legacy

John and Judy Wright Scholarship

Their years of advocacy and care inspired the John and Judy Wright Scholarship for Foster Children, established with the Ennis Center for Children to support foster youth in higher education — a direct continuation of the work the memoir documents.

About the Author

Judy Wright

Judy Wright is a foster mother, caregiver, and advocate whose life has been shaped by the demanding, ordinary work of staying. Over thirty-seven years of marriage to her late husband, John — a Vietnam veteran and corporate pilot — she lived out the conviction that children matter not abstractly, but concretely, in the daily decisions of a household.

After raising four children of their own, Judy and John entered Michigan's foster care system. Over twelve years in Oakland County, their home became a place of readiness for fifty-five children, many of them medically fragile. Judy learned to advocate inside systems that often failed to see children clearly, and to love without guarantees.

In 2012, they were named Oakland County Foster Parents of the Year by the Oakland County Board of Commissioners and Michigan Foster Parents of the Year — recognized by the Michigan Supreme Court — for their sustained commitment to medically complex and high-need placements. Their legacy includes the John and Judy Wright Scholarship for Foster Children, established in partnership with the Ennis Center for Children to support foster youth in higher education.

After John's death, Judy turned to writing. Just Hold Her is her debut memoir, drawn from the spiral notebooks she kept during a two-year vigil for a medically fragile infant in Michigan's foster care system. She writes for foster, medical, and faith-aware communities at Judy's Notebook.
